180 Estate Winery is a great place to enjoy wines in the Niagara region. But an experienced UX designer close to the owners noticed something that could affect the business in the long run, their website. According to his brief assessment it needed some improvements in the user interface and user experience, so he decided to do them a favor and gave his team of designers the mission of redesigning their website.

I was part of this team, and full of excitement we gathered to decide the overall plan to approach this project. The result was as shown below.

We chose to do a heuristic evaluation of the site, and merge its results with those of a usability test with similar user goals. The most important patterns of this combination were going to tell us what to fix during the redesign, and we were going to put that new design under test to outline the final conclusions and future steps for the project.

Why?

Because with the first usability testing we would validate our results from the heuristic evaluation. This would avoid making decisions based on our thinking rather than on real users’ opinions as it should be in user centered design. And with the second usability test we would refine our design even more for a future occasion if allowed.

The user goals that we picked covered the main motives that bring users to the site, purchasing wine and booking events. For the heuristic evaluation we went more granular to increase the probabilities of us finding violations to the usability principles, while in the usability test we went broader to give more freedom to the users, this would allow them to show us problems that we weren't aware of.

We did the heuristic evaluation and selected only the most important issues (most severe and most common amongst us). And we performed the usability tests with 8 users in total and grouped the notes into 7 themes.

Data Analysis

After having collected the data, we merged it as mentioned before. We used the usability testing themes as main categories to make sure we were focused on the user. In other words, we attached heuristic evaluation issues to the usability testing themes where they would fit in. And then we prioritized these new groups and decided which were going to dictate the changes in the website, while the others would be saved for later.

Why?

Because once again, we wanted to give more value to the opinion of the users than to our designers' opinions, this would make the process user centric. We wanted to fix the most severe findings first to avoid big problems, and we wanted to tackle the easy to fix findings first to generate more changes than otherwise generated.

Results

The themes that users pointed out the most were about unclear or absent information, confusion in the contact form, and confusion about terminology. While the ones they didn’t give much attention to were bad UX practices that we spotted as designers, these were about navigation, shopping cart, and Login functionalities.

We used the themes and criteria to decide the changes that we were going to implement in the website.

Here is a summary of the changes that we decided to implement based on the data analysis results:

  • Add a Login button
  • Add a Search bar
  • Increase contrast on elements
  • Enhance feedback visibility
  • Enhance navigation indicators
  • Improve confirmation messages
  • Put the calendar in a dropdown
  • Make checkboxes mutually exclusive
  • Make optional fields optional

    Redesign

    Finally, it was time to redesign. But each of us had secondary projects, so we came up with an approach to optimize our time and meet the deadline.

    This approach consisted of having a main designer and 3 reviewers for each redesign task, and rotating these roles so everyone had the main role at some point. This would make sure that the ideas of all of us were taken into consideration, and that each of us had enough time to dedicate to their secondary projects.

    The redesign tasks that we selected were:

  • Sitemap Redesign
  • Paper Prototyping
  • Low Fidelity Wireframing
  • High Fidelity Wireframing and Interactive Prototyping

    • Why?

    Because we considered that a sitemap enhancement is essential in the redesign of a website, and since that is an overarching change, we decided to do it first. After that, we could do a paper prototype implementing this new sitemap and the selected changes from the data analysis, and move to low and then high fidelity wireframes and prototype with group revisions after each one, meaning that we had a 3 layer refinement process that should guarantee a good design.

    I was in charge of the High Fidelity Wireframe and Interactive Prototype.

    Testing

    After completing the redesign process we performed usability testing with 8 new users, with the same user goals from the previous usability test, and wrote down the notes (We recruited 8 new participants to avoid affecting the results due to users that had already experienced the website). And finally we analysed the data by grouping similar findings like we did previously.
