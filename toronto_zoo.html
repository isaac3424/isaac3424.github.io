"Several groups of UX designers participated in a challenge about modifying Toronto Zoo’s website in two aspects, the image, and a user flow of our choice."

Our first decision after a few meetings was to not just modify a user flow in isolation, but also its connectors, that is, the navbar options.

Why?

To take an overall approach that would improve the entire experience and not just the main section of it because at the end that’s better for the user.

We also selected the user flow of making a donation by adopting an animal because it was long enough to give sufficient work for our team.

And we picked card sorting, user flow analysis, user testing, and usability testing as our UX techniques, where the modification of the look and feel would happen before the usability tests and would be based on the aesthetic sense of the designer in charge.

Why?

Because card sorting is the best choice for navbars and information architecture modifications as it changes terminologies and groupings based on the perspective of users. This would come first as navbars are the gateways to websites. Then would come analysing the user flow and making changes accordingly, prototyping and doing user testing on the mid-fidelity prototype, renewing the look and feel for the high-fidelity prototype, and performing usability testing on it at the end because this would guarantee feedback on all the work done.

Card Sorting

My team was composed of 6 designers and we decided to recruit 2 participants each for the card sorting activity, making a total of 12 participants. This was considered a good amount since more participants could start repeating the patterns.

Data Analysis

For the data analysis we just had an extensive meeting where everyone came with their participants' results analysed to share them and try to find similarities and common patterns. There were also cases where most of the results differed and it was quite challenging to make decisions on that. But luckily we did, here is the final sitemap in the way it resulted and the rationale of our decisions.

Rationale

As we were expecting, participants grouped all the animals and plants together, so we created a new category called Things to See and put the animals and plants there, because when someone goes to the Zoo he/she will be thinking about things that he/she can see.

The Your Safety First card was mostly grouped with Welfare and Sample your Product by the participants. But we decided to put it in the Things to do category as it gives information about how to treat animals which is something the visitors will do at the zoo.

We added the About Us category to put all the sections with information about the zoo that were grouped together at card sorting, but we rearranged them a little bit by placing Locations, Directions and Accessibility under Visit Us because this is the information users want to know when they are planning to visit the zoo. Also, it avoids having too many options at level 1.

We changed the name Welfare to Animal Welfare because the meaning is vague at first sight, but we discovered that it’s about how the zoo takes care of animals. So we changed it as Animal Welfare and placed it under About Us as it relates to the animals of the Zoo.

Media Center and Live Cams were together in the card sorts but we put Media Center as the parent category because Media is a broader term that encompasses Live Cams, a real-time video stream.

Sign Up, Log In, and Forgot Password were usually together in the card sorts and they are also together in most of the websites, so we labeled them as Account Access (we put the Login as a Login icon in the top navbar instead of as text since that is the convention).

We chose certain items to show in the footer based on our belief that they are important but they can be overlooked by users, or because users need to be reminded of them when they reach the bottom of the page because they are related to the business goals of the zoo. These items were: About Us, Contact Us, Donate, Animals, Tickets, Plan Your Visit, Help, FAQ, and Staff.

User Flow

Then came the part of analysing the user flow. As mentioned before, we picked the donate through adopting an animal flow. And due to the many possibilities available inside the flow we had to select specific ones for simplicity purposes at wireframing, prototyping and conducting usability testing. We chose the Jaguar as the animal and a monthly donation.

After this, everyone drew a user flow diagram according to his/her interpretation, we compared them and picked the best one, that is, the most efficient (with less ramifications) and more clear. We analysed it but didn’t find major problems so we kept it as it was and only used it as reference for creating the prototype that is described in the next section.

Prototyping

In prototyping we created a paper prototype and the mid-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes.

We all worked in each of these phases together, but dividing the load. Half of the group worked on mobile and the other one on desktop, with a mobile first approach for convenience, and alternating platforms as we moved from phase to phase.

We used mid-fidelity wireframes to move faster to high-fidelity due to the high amount of work and little available time, and we could go directly to mid-fidelity by using free assets for icons and other elements available on the Internet that simplified our work.

User Testing

We conducted user testing with 3 users and noticed that they sometimes clicked options that they didn’t have to click, and skipped many steps of the form process. But when asked about their opinion they didn’t give negative comments.

Usability Testing

Last but not least, we conducted usability testing using the think aloud method with 2 users for each of us making a total of 12 again to hone our design for the future. The results were as follows:

  • The Donations button was hard to find.

    • Reflection

    We got feedback like this one at Usability Testing despite good User Testing. Dive deeper in User Testing or do 2 rounds of Usability Testing.

    Next Steps

    Make the Donation button easier to find at the navigation bar. (High Priority)

  • Participants didn’t see the connection between Adopting an Animal and Donating for the zoo.

    • Reflection

    We didn’t dare to change unique ideas, terms, and relations of the website to keep its identity. We should have been less afraid.

    Next Steps

    Use consistent wording throughout the donation process, avoid the term "Adopt an Animal". (High Priority)

  • Participants didn’t see the need for a Dedication in the Donation.

    • Reflection

    We didn’t dare to change unique ideas, terms, and relations of the website to keep its identity. We should have been less afraid.

    Next Steps

    Simplify the Dedication page or remove it. (Low Priority)

  • Copy was sometimes considered incongruous or heavy.

    • Reflection

    We didn’t pay much attention to small details. Meeting the deadline is important but small details too.

    Next Steps

    Summarize the instructions on the left panel and make sure they correspond to the actions on the right. (Low Priority)

  • Sizes of text or form elements were too small for some participants.

    • Reflection

    We didn’t pay much attention to small details. Meeting the deadline is important but small details too.

    Next Steps

    Increase the size of the Monthly Donation button and Form Elements. (High Priority)

  • A lack of Back buttons was pointed out.

    • Reflection

    We didn’t pay much attention to small details. Meeting the deadline is important but small details too.

    Next Steps

    Configure the Back buttons. (High Priority)
